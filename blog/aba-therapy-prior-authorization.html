ABA Therapy Prior Authorization: How to Get Approved Faster

By ABA Therapy Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 9 min read

Prior authorization is the single biggest administrative barrier in ABA therapy. It delays treatment starts, consumes BCBA clinical time on paperwork instead of patient care, and when denied, leaves families without access to medically necessary services. But the process is predictable — and practices that understand what payers want can dramatically improve their approval rates and speed.

What Prior Authorization Requires

Prior authorization for ABA therapy is the payer's process for reviewing and approving treatment before it begins (or before an existing authorization is renewed). Nearly all payers — commercial, Medicaid, and TRICARE — require prior authorization for ABA services. The authorization request typically must include:

  • Autism diagnosis from a qualified diagnostician (not the treating BCBA)
  • Comprehensive behavioral assessment (97151) including standardized assessment results, FBA findings, and treatment recommendations
  • Individualized treatment plan with measurable goals, baseline data, and recommended hours by CPT code
  • Medical necessity justification explaining why the recommended intensity and duration of treatment is appropriate for this specific client
  • Provider credentials (BCBA certification, state license, NPI)

Why Authorizations Get Denied

Insufficient Medical Necessity Documentation

This is the #1 denial reason. The payer agrees that ABA is a covered benefit but determines that the documentation does not support the requested hours or duration. Common deficiencies include treatment plans with vague goals that aren't measurable, missing baseline data that would demonstrate the severity of the client's needs, assessment reports that describe deficits without quantifying them, and recommended hours that aren't justified by the clinical presentation.

Missing or Incomplete Assessment

Authorization requests submitted without a complete 97151 assessment — including standardized assessment scores, functional behavior assessment data, and a comprehensive treatment plan — are frequently denied or returned for additional information.

Diagnosis Issues

The autism diagnosis must come from a qualified diagnostician (psychologist, developmental pediatrician, or psychiatrist in most states). A BCBA cannot diagnose autism. If the diagnostic report is missing, expired, or from an unqualified provider, the authorization will be denied.

Provider Credentialing Gaps

If the BCBA or the practice is not properly credentialed with the payer, the authorization request will be rejected before clinical review even begins. Ensure credentialing is complete and active before submitting authorization requests.

The 80/20 rule of authorizations: Approximately 80% of ABA prior authorization denials stem from documentation quality issues, not coverage disputes. The payer isn't saying "ABA isn't covered" — they're saying "you haven't shown us enough evidence that this client needs this many hours." The fix is better documentation, not appeals.

How to Write Authorization Requests That Get Approved

Quantify Everything

Don't say "client has significant deficits in communication." Say "client demonstrates a manding repertoire of 12 independent mands (VB-MAPP Level 1, Milestone 5: 3/5), scoring below age expectations by 24+ months." Numbers give reviewers objective data to justify approval.

Connect Hours to Goals

Break down your recommended hours by code and explain how each component supports specific treatment goals. Example: "20 hours/week 97153 (direct therapy targeting 8 active skill acquisition programs and 3 behavior reduction targets), 2 hours/week 97155 (BCBA supervision for protocol modification across all targets), 1 hour/week 97156 (parent training on feeding protocol and functional communication at home)."

Include Functional Impact

Payer reviewers are influenced by functional impact documentation. Describe how the client's behavioral deficits affect their daily life — safety risks, inability to participate in school or community activities, family stress, and caregiver burden. This humanizes the numbers and strengthens the medical necessity argument.

Use the Payer's Criteria

Most payers publish their medical necessity criteria for ABA therapy. Find and read the specific payer's ABA policy before writing the authorization request, and address every criterion in their checklist explicitly. If the payer uses a specific medical necessity review tool (many use proprietary guidelines), structure your documentation to align with their framework.

Re-Authorization: The Renewal Process

Authorization renewals (typically every 3-6 months) require updated documentation showing progress on authorized treatment goals with quantified data, updated standardized assessment scores compared to baseline and prior assessment, revised treatment plan with new or modified goals, continued medical necessity justification for the requested hours, and explanation of any changes in recommended service intensity.

The most common reason for reduced hours at re-authorization is insufficient progress documentation. If your progress reports don't show measurable improvement or don't explain why progress is slower than expected, the payer will reduce authorized hours.

Appealing Authorization Denials

When an authorization is denied, you typically have 30-60 days to appeal (check the denial letter for exact timelines). Effective appeals include a detailed letter addressing the specific denial reason, additional clinical documentation that was missing from the original request, peer-reviewed literature supporting the recommended treatment intensity for the client's presentation, and a request for peer-to-peer review (a phone call between the treating BCBA and the payer's clinical reviewer).

Peer-to-peer reviews are often the most effective appeal step — a conversation between clinicians can resolve documentation concerns more efficiently than paper appeals.

The Bottom Line

Prior authorization is frustrating but manageable. The practices that maintain high approval rates and fast turnaround times are the ones that treat the authorization request as a clinical document, not a bureaucratic checkbox. Quantified assessments, measurable goals, clear hour justifications, and alignment with the payer's specific criteria are the formula. Invest the time in writing strong initial authorizations — it saves exponentially more time than fighting denials after the fact.
