How Much Does ABA Therapy Cost? With and Without Insurance

By ABA Therapy Billing Clarity  ·  April 2026  ·  9 min read

Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) therapy is the most widely recognized and evidence-based treatment for autism spectrum disorder. It is also one of the most expensive. Understanding the true cost of ABA therapy — and how insurance changes the equation — is essential for families making treatment decisions and for BCBA practice owners setting sustainable pricing.

ABA Therapy Cost Without Insurance

Without insurance coverage, ABA therapy typically costs between $120 and $250 per hour depending on the provider, location, and type of service. Because ABA programs commonly involve 20 to 40 hours of therapy per week, the annual cost without insurance can range from $62,000 to over $250,000.

Cost varies by service type:

  • Initial assessment (CPT 97151) — $150 to $300 per hour. Assessments typically require 3-8 hours total for the Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA) and treatment plan development.
  • Direct therapy by technician (CPT 97153) — $50 to $100 per hour. This is the bulk of ABA treatment, delivered by Registered Behavior Technicians (RBTs) under BCBA supervision.
  • BCBA supervision (CPT 97155) — $100 to $200 per hour. Direct BCBA observation and treatment modification during sessions.
  • Caregiver training (CPT 97156) — $100 to $175 per hour. Parent/caregiver guidance on implementing behavior strategies at home.

Why the range is so wide: ABA therapy cost is driven primarily by BCBA and RBT hourly rates, which vary dramatically by geography. A BCBA in rural Mississippi earns $60,000-$70,000 annually, while a BCBA in San Francisco may earn $100,000-$130,000. Those labor cost differences flow directly into the price of therapy.

ABA Therapy Cost With Insurance

With insurance coverage, families typically pay between $0 and $50 per session in copays or coinsurance after meeting their deductible. The insurer pays the remainder directly to the ABA provider at the contracted rate.

Employer-Sponsored Plans

Most large employer health plans now cover ABA therapy as a required benefit under autism mandate laws. Typical out-of-pocket costs include copays of $20-$50 per visit or 10-20% coinsurance after deductible. Annual out-of-pocket maximums (typically $3,000-$8,000 for families) cap total spending regardless of how many hours of ABA are used.

Medicaid

Medicaid covers ABA therapy in all 50 states following the 2014 CMS guidance requiring coverage of medically necessary treatments for EPSDT-eligible children. Medicaid patients typically have no copay or a nominal $1-$5 copay per visit. However, Medicaid reimbursement rates to providers are significantly lower than commercial rates — often 40-60% less — which limits provider availability.

Medicare

Medicare does not currently cover ABA therapy. ABA is classified as a habilitative service, and Medicare's coverage of habilitative therapy is extremely limited. This primarily affects adults with autism who age into Medicare.

State Autism Insurance Mandates

As of 2026, all 50 states plus the District of Columbia have enacted some form of autism insurance mandate that requires coverage of ABA therapy. However, these mandates vary significantly in their details — some cap coverage at a dollar amount (e.g., $50,000/year), some cap by age (e.g., coverage ends at age 18 or 21), and some have eliminated caps entirely.

Self-funded employer plans (ERISA plans) are exempt from state mandates. However, the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA) requires these plans to cover ABA if they cover other behavioral health services at the same level. In practice, most large self-funded plans now cover ABA.

What Affects the Price for Providers

For BCBA practice owners, understanding your cost structure is critical for sustainable pricing:

  • RBT labor costs — Your largest expense. RBT hourly wages range from $16 to $30+ depending on market. With benefits, taxes, and overhead, the fully loaded cost per RBT hour is typically 1.5-2x the base wage.
  • Supervision ratios — Most state and payer requirements mandate BCBA supervision at 5-10% of total direct service hours. Higher supervision requirements increase cost but also support clinical quality.
  • Administrative overhead — Prior authorizations, progress reports, treatment plan updates, and insurance billing consume significant non-billable hours. Practices that don't account for this in pricing will struggle financially.
  • Payer mix — Practices heavily dependent on Medicaid face tighter margins due to lower reimbursement rates. A diversified payer mix helps balance revenue per hour.

How to Reduce ABA Therapy Costs

For Families

  • Verify insurance benefits before starting therapy — call the number on your card and ask specifically about ABA/applied behavior analysis coverage
  • Ask about sliding scale fees if you're uninsured — some providers adjust rates based on income
  • Check whether your state's Medicaid program covers ABA for your child's age group
  • Use HSA/FSA funds for copays and deductible expenses — ABA is an eligible expense

For Providers

  • Negotiate payer contracts annually — don't accept initial rate offers without negotiation
  • Reduce claim denial rates through better documentation (this directly protects revenue)
  • Track authorization utilization — unused authorized hours represent unrealized revenue
  • Consider group therapy (CPT 97154) where clinically appropriate — it improves per-hour revenue while maintaining outcomes

The Bottom Line

ABA therapy is expensive — but for most families, insurance now covers the majority of the cost. The key is understanding your specific coverage, verifying benefits before starting therapy, and working with a provider that handles insurance billing efficiently. For providers, sustainable pricing requires accounting for the full cost of service delivery including supervision, administration, and compliance — not just the hourly wage of the treating technician.
