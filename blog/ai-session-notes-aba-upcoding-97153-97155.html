AI Session Notes, 97153 Units, and the Coming ABA Audit Wave

Blue Cross Blue Shield published the first hard data on AI-driven hospital upcoding in March 2026 — $2.3 billion in excess spending over three years. ABA providers weren't in the sample, but the industry is already under active enforcement. The Wall Street Journal's late-2025 cover story on $185 million in improper Medicaid ABA payments across three states was the warning shot. AI session-note tools in ABA are accelerating the exact patterns Medicaid Fraud Control Units are built to detect.

Why ABA Is the Most Exposed Applied Behavior Specialty

ABA billing is unit-based, high-frequency, and documentation-heavy. A single client can generate 20-40 units of 97153 per week, plus 97155 supervision, plus 97151 assessments. Each unit is 15 minutes. Each unit requires documented start-stop times, a treatment plan goal being addressed, the specific behavioral technique used, and the response data collected.

AI session-note tools promise to generate compliant narratives from technician data entry, but the compliance depends entirely on the data that goes in. When a tech enters "ran DTT for math" and the AI produces three paragraphs of detailed session narrative around goal progression, the documentation looks thorough — but the underlying observation may not support the billed units.

Where AI Drift Shows Up in ABA

  • 97153 unit inflation. Medicaid MFCUs have documented cases where sessions billed at 8 units (120 minutes) had documentation that supported 5 units. AI notes that fill narrative space across a stated time block without verifying actual direct-service minutes drive this pattern.
  • 97155 BCBA supervision billing during technician service. 97155 can be billed concurrently with 97153 in some states but not others, and only when specific BCBA activities occurred. AI-generated notes that describe generic "supervision" without BCBA-specific clinical actions fail concurrent-billing review.
  • 97151 assessment time. Limited to specific activities and billed at specific frequencies. AI notes that describe "ongoing assessment" woven into treatment time produce concurrent-billing audit flags.
  • 97156 caregiver training. Requires the caregiver to be present and actively participating. AI notes that describe "caregiver informed of progress" rather than actual training do not support the code.
  • Telehealth vs in-person coding. Modifier 95 and place of service 02/10 must match the actual delivery mode. AI scribe templates from prior sessions can carry forward incorrect telehealth modifiers after a client returns to in-clinic service.

What State Medicaid Programs Are Already Doing

Medicaid MFCUs in Indiana, Arizona, and Tennessee — the states featured in the WSJ 2025 cover story — are running analytics on ABA billing patterns at the provider level. High-unit providers, high-97155 concurrent billers, and providers with outlier weekly unit counts per client are being pulled for documentation review. The review looks for exactly the gap BCBS documented in hospitals: billed intensity that exceeds documented service.

Commercial payers — including UnitedHealthcare Community Plan, Centene subsidiaries, and Molina — are using the same analytic patterns. A practice with a 24-unit-per-week average per client, in a region where peers average 18, is routinely receiving targeted probe samples.

A Five-Chart Self-Audit You Can Run This Week

  1. Pull five recent sessions billed at 6+ units of 97153.
  2. For each, verify the session start-stop times, the technician sign-in/sign-out data, and the treatment plan goals addressed.
  3. Check every 97155 billed concurrently with 97153: does the note describe BCBA-specific clinical activity, not just oversight?
  4. Review 97151 assessments from the last 30 days: is the time billed bounded to assessment activities, or woven through treatment?
  5. Spot-check telehealth modifiers: do POS codes match the actual service location?

The Bottom Line

ABA was already under enforcement pressure before the BCBS findings. The combination of high-unit billing, high-frequency sessions, and AI-generated narratives is the most audit-exposed configuration in all of outpatient healthcare right now. The providers who don't are the next WSJ cover story.
