ABA Therapy OIG Enforcement 2026: What Auditors Are Looking For Right Now

April 2026  ·  ABA Therapy Billing Clarity  ·  8 min read

The Office of Inspector General of the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services has made ABA therapy billing a priority enforcement area. With billions of dollars in Medicaid ABA claims processed annually and significant program growth since autism insurance mandates took effect across all 50 states, federal and state enforcement agencies have increased their focus on ABA billing practices. For BCBA practice owners, understanding what auditors are looking for is the first step in protecting your practice.

Why ABA Has Become an OIG Priority

The rapid growth of ABA therapy as a billable service — from a niche specialty to a mainstream behavioral health benefit — has attracted both legitimate providers and bad actors. Several factors have drawn OIG attention to the sector:

  • High claims volume and dollar amounts: ABA is one of the highest-cost behavioral health benefits, with intensive programs billing 20–40 hours per week per patient. This creates significant financial exposure for Medicaid programs.
  • Complex billing codes with supervision requirements: The 2019 introduction of CPT codes 97151–97158 created new compliance requirements that not all providers have fully implemented.
  • Rapid provider growth: The number of ABA providers has grown substantially, and rapid growth in any healthcare sector historically draws enforcement scrutiny.
  • Documented fraud in prior enforcement actions: High-profile enforcement actions against ABA providers have demonstrated that the sector is vulnerable to both intentional fraud and unintentional compliance failures at scale.

The Patterns That Trigger OIG Investigations

OIG investigations and Medicaid audits of ABA providers are typically triggered by data analytics that identify statistical outliers — providers whose billing patterns differ significantly from their peers. Understanding these patterns helps practices audit their own billing before regulators do.

Billing for Services Not Rendered

The most serious enforcement cases involve billing for services that never occurred. Auditors cross-reference claims data with provider and patient records looking for claims where documented records contradict the billed service — including GPS data, school attendance records, or other documentation showing the patient or provider was elsewhere during the claimed service time.

Upcoding

Billing BCBA-level codes (particularly 97155 — Adaptive Behavior Treatment with Protocol Modification) for services actually delivered by technicians at the 97153 level. This can happen through intentional miscoding or through inadequate understanding of which code applies to which provider type and service.

Excessive Billing Compared to Peers

Providers who consistently bill significantly more hours per patient than their regional peers draw statistical flags. This doesn't indicate fraud on its own, but it triggers closer review of whether the documented services actually occurred and whether authorizations support the billed volume.

Billing During Documented Absences

Claims submitted for dates when school records, hospitalization records, or other documentation shows the patient was unavailable are a common audit finding. Practices should have processes to catch and void claims for sessions that didn't occur due to patient absence.

Supervision Documentation Deficiencies

Billing 97153 without adequate documentation that BCBA supervision is occurring at the frequency required by the payer is one of the most common compliance findings — and in cases where supervision documentation is entirely absent, it can support an allegation that supervision was not occurring at all.

Credential-Based Billing Issues

Billing for services delivered by providers who were not credentialed with the payer at the time of service, or billing BCBA-level codes for providers who are not BCBAs, are common findings that can lead to recoupment demands covering long periods of service.

Important: Many ABA enforcement actions have targeted practices where compliance failures were unintentional — the result of inadequate systems rather than deliberate fraud. The False Claims Act standard does not require intent to defraud; "reckless disregard" for billing accuracy can create liability.

State Medicaid Integrity Program Audits

In addition to federal OIG activity, state Medicaid Integrity Programs (MIPs) conduct their own audits of ABA providers. These state-level audits are often data-driven and may result in prepayment review — where claims are held pending documentation review — or post-payment audits resulting in recoupment demands.

State Medicaid audits typically focus on:

  • Whether prior authorization was in place for all billed services
  • Whether documentation supports the units billed (particularly for time-based codes like 97153)
  • Whether supervision requirements were met and documented
  • Whether the diagnosis and medical necessity documentation support the services provided

What a Compliance-Focused Practice Does Differently

The practices that navigate OIG and Medicaid scrutiny successfully are those that have built documentation and billing compliance into their daily operations. Key differentiators include:

Internal Audit Process

Conducting regular internal audits of a random sample of claims — comparing what was billed against the supporting documentation — allows practices to identify compliance gaps before external auditors do. Monthly audits of 10–15 randomly selected claims are more effective than annual reviews.

Session Note Quality Control

Implementing a supervisory review of session notes before claims are submitted creates a checkpoint that catches documentation deficiencies. Particular attention should be paid to start/end times, data documentation, and supervision references.

Contemporaneous Documentation

Documentation completed at the time of service is significantly more defensible than retroactively completed records. Practices that allow technicians to complete session notes days after sessions are creating audit vulnerability — both because late notes raise questions and because accuracy decays quickly.

Clear Supervision Records

Maintaining detailed supervision logs that document when supervision occurred, what was reviewed, and any protocol modifications discussed provides evidence that the supervision requirement underlying 97153 billing was actually met.

Proactive Compliance Monitoring

Staying current with OIG Work Plan updates, CMS guidance, and payer policy bulletins allows practices to identify new enforcement priorities before audits begin. The OIG publishes its Work Plan priorities publicly, and monitoring this guidance is the most direct way to understand where enforcement attention is focused.
