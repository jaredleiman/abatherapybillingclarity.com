ABA Therapy Billing Compliance: What Every BCBA Practice Needs to Know in 2026

By ABA Therapy Billing Clarity  ·  April 2026  ·  8 min read

ABA therapy billing is among the most complex and heavily scrutinized specialties in behavioral healthcare. Since the 2019 introduction of the Category I CPT codes for applied behavior analysis, the billing landscape has shifted dramatically — with the OIG, commercial payers, and state Medicaid programs all intensifying oversight of ABA claims.

For BCBA practice owners, the compliance burden is significant. Unlike many specialties, ABA billing requires specialized knowledge of behavior-analytic service definitions, supervision ratio documentation, and payer-specific authorization requirements that change frequently and without warning. This guide covers the core compliance requirements every ABA practice needs to understand in 2026.

Why ABA Billing Draws More Scrutiny Than Other Specialties

Applied behavior analysis is unique in the healthcare billing landscape for several reasons that combine to create elevated audit risk:

  • Volume and intensity: ABA is often delivered at 20–40 hours per week per client, generating significantly higher claim volume than most outpatient specialties.
  • Rapid industry growth: Since autism coverage mandates spread across all 50 states, ABA billing volume has grown dramatically — drawing attention from payers and enforcement agencies alike.
  • Documentation complexity: Every claim requires evidence of BCBA supervision, session-level notes tied to treatment plans, and authorization verification — multiple points of potential non-compliance.
  • OIG high-risk designation: The OIG has explicitly identified ABA as a high-risk billing area, citing concerns about services not rendered, upcoding, and inadequate supervision documentation.

Key fact: The OIG pursued multiple significant ABA enforcement actions between 2024 and 2026, with recoupments ranging from hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars per provider. Most stemmed from documentation failures, not intentional fraud.

The Core CPT Code Set: 97151–97158

All ABA billing is built around eight Category I CPT codes. Understanding what each requires — and where audit risk concentrates — is the foundation of compliance.

97151 — Behavior Identification Assessment

The intake assessment code, billed once per episode of care. Requires direct BCBA time and must be supported by a functional behavior assessment. Payers scrutinize this code for overbilling and inadequate FBA documentation.

97153 — Adaptive Behavior Treatment by Protocol

The highest-volume ABA code. Technician-delivered treatment under a BCBA-designed protocol. Each unit is 15 minutes. Auditors focus heavily on unit count accuracy, session note quality, and supervision ratio compliance.

97155 — Adaptive Behavior Treatment with Protocol Modification

BCBA direct treatment time. Requires documentation demonstrating the BCBA was directly modifying protocols — not just supervising technicians.

97156 — Family Adaptive Behavior Treatment Guidance

Caregiver training delivered by a BCBA or BCaBA. Frequently underbilled — many practice owners do not realize this service is separately billable under its own code.

The Three Pillars of ABA Billing Compliance

1. Authorization Management

Prior authorization is required for virtually all ABA services across Medicaid and commercial payers. Authorization failures are the leading cause of ABA claim denials. Key requirements:

  • Obtain authorization before services begin — retroactive authorizations are rarely granted
  • Track authorized hours by CPT code, not just total hours
  • Monitor expiration dates and initiate re-authorization at least 30 days before expiration
  • Document medical necessity at each re-authorization with updated assessment data

2. Session Documentation

Every billable session requires contemporaneous documentation supporting the service billed. Payers and auditors look for session start and end times, specific behavioral targets addressed, data collection tied to the treatment plan, therapist credentials, and BCBA oversight documentation.

3. Supervision Ratio Compliance

BCBA supervision of RBTs and BCaBAs is both a clinical and billing requirement. Most payers require a specific supervision ratio — commonly 1 BCBA to 6–10 RBTs — but this varies by payer and state. Supervision must be documented with dates, duration, and the supervisee's caseload covered.

Common error: Many practices track supervision informally. Payers expect written supervision logs that can be produced on request during an audit. Undocumented supervision is treated the same as no supervision.

Common Audit Triggers to Avoid

OIG and payer audits in ABA are most commonly triggered by statistical anomalies in claims data. Practices that bill significantly above peer norms for any of these metrics draw attention:

  • Average weekly hours per client above 30 hours per week
  • Percentage of claims for 97153 vs. BCBA-level codes outside expected ratios
  • Rapid growth in claim volume without corresponding growth in credentialed staff
  • High rates of billing on evenings and weekends without documented clinical justification
  • Authorization utilization rates consistently at or above 100%
