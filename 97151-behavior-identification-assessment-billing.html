97151 Behavior Identification Assessment: Billing Rules, Documentation, and Audit Risk

By ABA Therapy Billing Clarity  ·  April 2026  ·  7 min read

CPT 97151 — Behavior Identification Assessment — is the gateway code for all ABA services. It is billed once per episode of care and establishes the clinical foundation that justifies every subsequent treatment claim. Without a properly documented 97151, the entire treatment record is vulnerable.

Despite being billed infrequently relative to treatment codes like 97153, CPT 97151 carries significant audit risk. Payers scrutinize it because errors here — overbilling, inadequate FBA documentation, or wrong provider type — can invalidate downstream authorizations and treatment claims retroactively.

What CPT 97151 Covers

CPT 97151 is defined as a behavior identification assessment conducted by a BCBA or BCaBA, face-to-face with a patient and/or caregiver. The assessment must include structured data collection and result in a written report that forms the basis of the behavioral intervention plan.

Key definitional requirements:

  • BCBA or BCaBA required: This code cannot be billed by an RBT. The assessment must be conducted by a Board Certified Behavior Analyst or Board Certified Assistant Behavior Analyst.
  • Billed once per episode of care: Not per authorization period, not annually. Once per episode — meaning once per treatment course for a given condition. Re-assessments are billed under 97151 only when a new episode of care begins.
  • Each unit is 30 minutes: Unlike treatment codes billed in 15-minute units, 97151 is billed in 30-minute units. A 90-minute assessment = 3 units.
  • Must produce a written assessment report: The assessment is not complete until a written report documenting findings, behavioral hypotheses, and treatment recommendations is finalized.

Critical point: "Once per episode of care" is interpreted differently by different payers. Most commercial payers treat it as once per authorization cycle. Medicaid programs vary. Always verify with each payer before re-billing 97151 for an existing patient.

The Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA) Requirement

The FBA is the clinical backbone of the 97151 assessment. Most payers require that the 97151 report include or reference a complete functional behavior assessment. What constitutes a complete FBA varies by payer, but core components universally expected include:

Indirect Assessment

Structured interviews and rating scales completed with caregivers, teachers, or other informants. Commonly used tools include the Functional Assessment Interview (FAI), the Questions About Behavioral Function (QABF), and various behavioral checklists. Indirect assessment alone is not sufficient for most payers.

Direct Observation

Systematic observation of the patient in their natural environment, documenting antecedents, behaviors, and consequences (ABC data). Most payers require direct observation across multiple settings or time periods. Single-session observations are frequently flagged as insufficient.

Functional Analysis (When Required)

Some payers — particularly for higher-intensity treatment requests — require a functional analysis (systematic experimental manipulation of environmental variables) as part of the 97151 assessment. This is distinct from a functional behavior assessment and requires BCBA-level expertise to conduct safely.

Behavioral Hypothesis

The FBA must conclude with a written behavioral hypothesis stating the function of the target behavior — attention, escape, access to tangibles, automatic reinforcement, or combinations. The hypothesis must be supported by the assessment data collected.

Documentation Required in the 97151 Report

The written 97151 assessment report is what payers review during authorization and audit. A complete report includes:

  • Patient identifying information and diagnosis documentation
  • Reason for referral and presenting concerns
  • Assessment methods used (indirect and direct) with dates of assessment
  • Description of target behaviors with operational definitions
  • Data from indirect assessments (interview summaries, rating scale scores)
  • Data from direct observation (frequency, duration, or ABC data)
  • Behavioral hypothesis with supporting data
  • Treatment recommendations including proposed CPT codes, weekly hours, and treatment goals
  • BCBA signature with credential number and date of completion

Common 97151 Billing Errors

Overbilling Units

The most common 97151 error. Because the assessment involves review time, report writing, and caregiver contact in addition to direct observation, some practices bill units for all time spent on assessment-related activities. Payers typically cover only face-to-face assessment time, not report-writing time. Check each payer's specific policy.

Wrong Provider Type

Billing 97151 for an assessment conducted by an RBT, even under BCBA supervision. The assessment itself must be conducted by a BCBA or BCaBA — not delegated to a technician. If an RBT administers any portion of the assessment, only the portions conducted by the BCBA/BCaBA are billable under 97151. Technician assessment time is billed under 97152.

Re-billing in the Same Episode

Billing 97151 again during an ongoing treatment episode without a new episode of care beginning. Some practices bill 97151 at each re-authorization, treating it as an annual assessment code. Most payers will deny this and may flag the practice for further review.

Insufficient FBA Documentation

Submitting a treatment plan or clinical summary in place of a true functional behavior assessment. Payers that review 97151 documentation look for the specific components outlined above. Narrative descriptions of behavior without structured assessment data are frequently rejected.

Payer-Specific 97151 Requirements

Beyond universal requirements, major payers impose additional conditions:

  • UnitedHealth: Requires the 97151 report to be submitted with the initial prior authorization request. Authorization will not be granted without it.
  • Aetna: Has specific requirements around the length and format of the FBA report, and may require a functional analysis for patients with severe challenging behavior.
  • Medicaid: Many state Medicaid programs require the 97151 assessment to be conducted by a BCBA who is enrolled as a Medicaid provider independently — not just through a group practice.
  • Tricare: Requires prior authorization for 97151 itself before the assessment is conducted. Many practices miss this and conduct the assessment before authorization is in place.

Tricare note: Unlike most payers, Tricare requires prior authorization for the 97151 assessment itself, not just for treatment. Conducting the assessment without prior authorization and then billing 97151 will result in denial.

97151 and the Authorization Chain

The 97151 report is not just a billing document — it is the clinical foundation that payers use to authorize all subsequent treatment. A weak or incomplete 97151 report creates downstream risk:

  • Insufficient justification for the intensity of treatment requested (weekly hours)
  • Inadequate behavioral hypotheses that don't support the proposed intervention strategies
  • Missing operational definitions that make treatment goals unmeasurable
  • Lack of baseline data that makes progress measurement impossible

When payers deny or reduce treatment authorizations, they frequently cite the quality of the initial 97151 assessment as justification. Investing time in a thorough, well-documented assessment pays dividends throughout the entire treatment episode.
