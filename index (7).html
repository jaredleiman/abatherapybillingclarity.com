97153 and 97155: How AI Downcoding Is Quietly Reducing ABA Therapy Reimbursement

10 min read · For independent ABA practice owners and BCBAs
Quick Answer

Insurers are using AI claim-review systems to silently downcode ABA claims through two main mechanisms: reducing the number of 97155 (BCBA protocol modification) units paid per session, and reclassifying 97155 supervision time as 97156 (family adaptive behavior treatment guidance), which pays substantially less per unit. The claim isn't denied — it's paid at a reduced amount, with no notice. The American Academy of Family Physicians called for a federal investigation in 2026, and several state legislatures introduced AI downcoding transparency bills. Independent ABA practices can respond by auditing remittance data for unit reductions and CPT shifts, restructuring BCBA documentation to clearly identify protocol modification activity, and submitting batch appeals when systematic patterns emerge.

A BCBA at your practice spends 90 minutes during a session: 60 minutes observing the technician deliver direct treatment under 97153, then 30 minutes actively modifying three skill acquisition programs based on the data — adjusting prompt levels on one, advancing to a new mastery criterion on another, and changing the reinforcement schedule on a third. The BCBA documents the protocol changes and bills six 15-minute units of 97155 (90 minutes of qualified-professional time).

Three weeks later, the EOB shows the claim was paid. Your billing team moves on. But when they reconcile units billed to units paid, they see the payer paid four units of 97155 — not six. About $80 less than expected, depending on the contract rate. No denial. No documentation request. Just a quiet reduction.

Or worse: the EOB shows the 97155 was reclassified to 97156 — paid at a much lower per-unit rate, costing the practice $30-40 per unit. Same 90 minutes of work, paid as if it were parent training instead of clinical protocol modification.

This is AI downcoding in ABA, and in 2026 it became one of the largest silent revenue leaks in independent practices.

The ABA CPT code family

Most ABA billing involves a small core of codes. Each has a specific provider type, activity definition, and per-unit reimbursement.

Code Activity Provider Type
97151 Behavior identification assessment BCBA / qualified professional
97153 Adaptive behavior treatment by protocol RBT / BT (technician)
97155 Adaptive behavior treatment with protocol modification BCBA / qualified professional
97156 Family adaptive behavior treatment guidance BCBA / qualified professional
97158 Group adaptive behavior treatment with protocol modification BCBA / qualified professional

AI downcoding in ABA hits 97155 hardest because it's the highest-paying recurring code on most practices' fee schedules and because the documentation distinguishing it from 97156 lives entirely in narrative — exactly the kind of input AI tools can re-evaluate.

How AI downcoding works in ABA

Two patterns dominate. They often appear together.

Pattern 1: Unit reduction on 97155

You bill six 15-minute units of 97155. The payer pays four. The reasoning embedded in the AI logic typically maps to one of two assumptions: either the documentation didn't justify all six units of protocol modification activity, or the AI inferred that some of the time billed under 97155 was actually direct supervision of the technician (which some payer policies don't reimburse separately under 97155).

The detection challenge: most ABA billing systems show the 97155 line as paid, with the unit count buried in the detail. Without an explicit billed-vs-paid units comparison, the loss is invisible.

Pattern 2: 97155 reclassified to 97156

You bill 97155 for BCBA protocol modification time. The payer reclassifies the line to 97156 (family guidance) and pays at the lower 97156 rate. The AI logic typically triggers this when the documentation includes any reference to family or caregiver interaction during the session — even when the primary activity was protocol modification, not family training.

Documentation that reads like a hybrid session is the highest-risk pattern. Documentation that clearly separates protocol modification activity from any caregiver consultation is much safer.

Why ABA is hit harder than most specialties

Three structural factors make ABA an unusually frequent AI downcoding target.

Service intensity is high and visible. A typical ABA case generates dozens of billed units per week across multiple CPT codes. AI tools have rich data to pattern-match on — the higher the volume of claims per patient, the easier it is for an algorithm to identify "expected" patterns and flag deviations.

Concurrent and sequential billing rules vary by payer. Some payers permit 97153 and 97155 to be billed concurrently when a BCBA is modifying protocols while a technician delivers direct care. Others require strictly non-concurrent time. AI tools are aggressive about flagging concurrent claims, often reducing units regardless of whether the payer's actual policy permits the concurrence.

Documentation distinguishes 97155 from 97156 entirely on narrative content. Unlike specialties where the difference between two codes is a binary documentation element (number of regions, number of body parts examined, time threshold), the difference between 97155 and 97156 lives in what activity is described. That makes ABA uniquely vulnerable to natural-language-processing-based downcoding.

The Pattern in Plain Numbers

An ABA practice with 30 active clients receiving roughly 20 hours per week of services — including 4 BCBA hours billed at 97155 — generates around 480 weekly 97155 units across the caseload. If the AI downcoding rate is 15% (some units reduced, some reclassified to 97156), the average loss per affected unit might be $25. That works out to $1,800 per week, or roughly $94,000 per year in revenue earned but never received. Larger practices with more BCBA-heavy programming see materially higher losses.

How to detect AI downcoding in your own practice

Detection requires three comparisons: units billed vs units paid (per CPT code), CPT submitted vs CPT paid (line by line), and rate paid vs contracted rate.

The 60-minute audit

  1. Pull your last 90 days of paid claims from MA plans and commercial payers, separated by payer. Most ABA practice management systems (CentralReach, Rethink, NPA Works, Theralytics) export this as a CSV.
  2. Filter to 97155 lines. Compare billed units to paid units. Count any line where they differ.
  3. Filter to 97156 lines on dates of service that also have 97155 billed. If any 97155 was reclassified to 97156, you'll often see this pattern: a 97155 line paid at zero units (or removed) and a 97156 line paid that wasn't on your original claim.
  4. Group results by payer. Often one or two payers drive most of the loss.

Run this for your top commercial payers and for each Medicaid managed care plan. The dollar impact per identified pattern is material — a single corrected unit interpretation across a high-volume payer can recover thousands per month going forward.

How to document 97155 to defend against AI downcoding

The fastest documentation upgrade most ABA practices can make: structure 97155 session notes to lead with protocol modification activity, with explicit identification of which programs were modified, what data drove the modification, and what was changed.

Instead of:

"BCBA observed session, provided supervision and protocol updates. Discussed treatment goals with mom."

Write:

"Protocol modifications during this 90-minute session: (1) Manding program — reviewed last 5 sessions of mand data; mastery criterion of 80% across 3 sessions met; advanced from echoic-to-mand transfer to spontaneous mand stage. (2) Receptive identification program — error pattern data showed 60% accuracy on 2D stimuli; reduced field size from 4 to 3 and added differential reinforcement of correct responses. (3) Tolerating delays program — increased delay duration from 5 to 8 seconds based on stable performance over the past 2 weeks. Brief end-of-session caregiver update on receptive program changes (5 minutes, not billed under 97156)."

This isn't longer. It's just structured to survive AI parsing. The note explicitly identifies clinical decision-making, concrete protocol changes, and the data supporting each — exactly what AI tools look for to keep 97155 from being downgraded.

How to appeal a downcoded ABA claim

Most payers have a 60-180 day appeal window. Three things make appeals win:

  • Documentation that maps explicitly to protocol modification. If the note already has program-by-program modification language, the appeal is straightforward. If not, the appeal is much weaker.
  • Reference to the payer's published medical policy. Most major commercial payers and Medicaid MCOs publish ABA coverage policies. Quote them. Make the payer prove their AI followed the policy.
  • Pattern documentation in the cover letter. "Our practice has identified [N] downcoded 97155 lines from [Payer] over the past [period], totaling [units] reduced and $[amount] in unrecovered revenue."

Single-claim appeals rarely justify the time investment in ABA, where unit values are modest. Batch appeals — submitting 50+ downcoded lines at once with a systematic-pattern cover letter — carry meaningful leverage because they signal regulatory complaint risk. Many state Medicaid programs and BHO contracts include language requiring payers to disclose AI involvement, which is often violated when downcoding is systematic.

What's coming in 2026

The legislative response is moving but unevenly. Several state bills proposed in 2026 would require payers to disclose AI involvement in claim adjudication; others would create a private right of action for systematic downcoding. None have become law yet. What's more likely in the next 6-12 months:

  • State Medicaid program scrutiny. ABA has high state-level oversight, and AI downcoding by Medicaid MCOs is exactly the kind of pattern state oversight bodies investigate.
  • Trade association advocacy. National and state ABA associations have signaled increasing attention to AI claim review.
  • Class action litigation from ABA practice groups. Multiple cases were filed against major commercial payers in 2025-2026.
  • Quiet payer policy revisions. AI thresholds get retuned without announcement when pressure builds. The only reliable detection is monitoring your remittance patterns over time.

None of this helps a practice getting downcoded today. What helps today is detection, documentation upgrades on 97155 specifically, and batch appeals on systematic patterns.

The bigger picture

AI downcoding by payers sits in a larger pattern that hit independent ABA practices in 2026: payers using automation to extract margin without changing the contract. It's the mirror image of AI upcoding stories at large health systems. Same technology, opposite direction of force. Independent ABA practices sit in the middle — without the AI tools that large systems use to push codes up, but absorbing the AI tools commercial and Medicaid payers use to push codes down. The only available defense is regular monitoring of your own remittance data — line-by-line, payer-by-payer, unit-by-unit.

The practices that catch this in 2026 are the ones treating remittance reconciliation as something to read, not just file.
